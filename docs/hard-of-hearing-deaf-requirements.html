Captions and transcripts

Captions are extremely important for anyone who may have difficulties understanding spoken language or sounds. This is not just people who are hard of hearing or D/deaf, but also people struggling to pay attention due to neurodivergence or situation, and those accessing the content in locations where the sound cannot/should not be heard.

"Open" captions are "baked-in" to the video content, and thus cannot be disabled or enabled. This also means their content can't be accessed by other means, such as a refreshable Braille display which may be used by people with both visual and hearing disabilities.

"Closed" captions (CC) can be enabled/disabled, but should be enabled by default. Not every platform will allow the use of CC, but it should be preferred whenever possible. The HTML <video> element has a track attribute, which can be used to provide a .vtt file with the associated captions.

Transcripts are the complete written record of the sound content of a piece of media. The speakers should be identified, as well as relevant sound effects, and transcripts should be available independent of progress through media playback.

Auto-generated captions can save time, but are rarely accurate. Human live captioners and human-created/reviewed transcripts are preferable, and vital if any media content being misunderstood could have significant negative impact.

Sign language

Spoken language is not always the first language of D/deaf people, and, due to grammatical structures of signed languages and spoken languages differing, captions are not necessarily the most accessible way for these users to access content. Providing signed interpreting is a Level AAA WCAG success criterion.

Jessica in the video above is using Sign Supported English (SSE) rather than BSL. This uses the signs of BSL but with the grammatical structure of English. This is often used by people who have become deaf later in life.

Additonally, while certain signs may overlap, signed languages used in countries that share a spoken language can be vastly different. American Sign Language (ASL) and British Sign Language (BSL), for example, differ hugely, with the former having far more in common with French Sign Language (LSF).

A note on language

People who are culturally Deaf (e.g. raised in a Deaf family) refer to themselves as "Deaf" with an uppercase "D". A person can be deaf but not Deaf, hence the usage of "D/deaf" here. Further explanation on this can be found at Inclusion London.
